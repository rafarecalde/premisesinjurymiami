Dog Bite Injuries on Miami Properties: Premises Liability and Owner Responsibility

By Rafael Recalde, Esq. • Miami Premises Injury Attorney

Dog bite injuries are among the most physically and emotionally devastating incidents that can occur on someone else's property. When a dog attacks a visitor, tenant, delivery worker, or child on a residential or commercial property in Miami, the consequences extend far beyond the initial wound. Victims face painful medical treatment, lasting scars, deep psychological trauma, and financial burdens that can persist for years. What many victims do not realize is that a dog bite on someone else's property is not just a matter between the victim and the dog's owner. It is a premises liability issue, and multiple parties may bear legal responsibility.

In Miami-Dade County, dog bite cases carry unique legal dimensions due to Florida's strict liability statute, local breed-specific legislation, and the dense mix of apartment complexes, condominiums, and rental properties where millions of residents live in close proximity to each other and their pets. If you or a loved one has been bitten by a dog on someone else's property, understanding who is liable and how to protect your claim is essential.

Florida's Strict Liability Dog Bite Statute

Florida Statute 767.01 establishes a strict liability framework for dog bite cases. This means that the owner of a dog is liable for damages suffered by any person bitten by the dog while in a public place or lawfully in a private place, including the property of the dog's owner. The critical distinction under Florida law is that the dog owner is liable regardless of whether they had any prior knowledge that the dog was dangerous or had ever bitten anyone before.

Unlike many other states that follow a "one bite rule," where the owner must have known or had reason to know the dog was aggressive, Florida eliminates that requirement entirely. The dog does not need a documented history of aggression. The owner does not need to have received prior complaints. If their dog bites someone who is lawfully present on the property, the owner is strictly liable for the resulting injuries.

This strict liability standard applies whether the bite occurs in the dog owner's backyard, in an apartment hallway, in a condominium common area, or anywhere else the victim has a legal right to be. However, the statute does allow for a reduction in liability if the victim's own negligence contributed to the bite, such as provoking the animal. The burden falls on the dog owner to prove that provocation occurred.

The Premises Liability Angle: When Property Owners Share Responsibility

While the dog's owner faces strict liability under Florida law, a dog bite on someone else's property opens an additional and often more significant avenue for compensation: premises liability claims against the property owner, landlord, property management company, or homeowners association. These claims are especially relevant in Miami, where a significant portion of the population lives in apartments, condominiums, and managed rental properties.

Landlord Liability for Dangerous Dogs

Landlords in Miami have a legal duty to maintain their rental properties in a reasonably safe condition for tenants, visitors, and guests. When a landlord knows or should know that a tenant's dog is dangerous and fails to take action, the landlord may be held liable for injuries that result. This liability can arise in several ways. A landlord who receives complaints from other tenants about an aggressive dog but takes no steps to address the situation has arguably breached their duty of care. A landlord who fails to enforce existing pet policies in the lease agreement, such as breed restrictions or weight limits, may also face liability. Properties with inadequate fencing, broken gates, or poorly maintained common areas that allow a dangerous dog to access shared spaces create additional grounds for a premises liability claim.

Many apartment complexes in Miami have specific pet policies that restrict certain breeds, require proof of vaccinations, and mandate that dogs be leashed in common areas. When property management fails to enforce these policies after receiving notice of a problem, their inaction becomes a basis for legal liability.

HOA Liability for Dog Attacks in Common Areas

Homeowners associations in Miami-Dade County bear responsibility for maintaining safe common areas including lobbies, hallways, elevators, pool decks, courtyards, and shared outdoor spaces. When a dog attack occurs in one of these common areas, the HOA may be liable if it failed to enforce its own pet regulations, ignored prior complaints about an aggressive animal, or neglected to maintain gates, fences, or other barriers designed to keep residents safe. HOAs that are aware of a dangerous dog in the community and take no steps to address the threat can face significant legal exposure when an attack occurs.

Property Management Company Responsibility

Many Miami apartment buildings and condominium complexes are operated by professional property management companies. These companies assume day-to-day responsibility for the safety of the property, including enforcing lease terms and community rules. When a property management company receives reports of an aggressive dog and fails to act, whether by issuing warnings, requiring the animal's removal, or taking other corrective measures, the management company itself may be named as a defendant in a dog bite premises liability claim. Their contractual obligation to manage the property safely creates a duty that, when breached, exposes them to liability.

Multiple Liable Parties: In a Miami dog bite case on a rental or condominium property, the dog's owner, the landlord, the property management company, and the HOA may all share liability. Identifying every responsible party is critical to maximizing your recovery, as each may carry separate insurance policies.

Miami-Dade County's Pit Bull Ban and Breed-Specific Legislation

Miami-Dade County maintains one of the most well-known breed-specific ordinances in the country. The county has banned pit bulls since 1989, making it illegal to own, keep, or harbor pit bull dogs within county limits. Violations carry fines, and the animal may be subject to confiscation and euthanasia. This ordinance significantly affects dog bite premises liability claims in the county.

When a dog bite involves a breed that is banned under Miami-Dade's ordinance, the dog owner is already in violation of local law simply by possessing the animal. This violation strengthens a victim's claim considerably, as it establishes that the owner was acting unlawfully. It also creates additional liability for landlords and property managers who knowingly or negligently allowed a banned breed on their property. A property owner who was aware that a tenant was keeping a pit bull in violation of the county ban and failed to act faces heightened legal exposure.

Even for breeds that are not banned, Miami-Dade County requires all dog owners to comply with leash laws and registration requirements. Violations of these ordinances can serve as evidence of negligence in a premises liability case.

Insurance Coverage for Dog Bite Injuries

One of the most important practical considerations in any dog bite case is insurance coverage. Dog bite claims are typically covered under the liability portion of homeowner's insurance and renter's insurance policies. Most standard homeowner's policies include personal liability coverage that extends to dog bite injuries, usually with limits ranging from one hundred thousand to three hundred thousand dollars or more. Renter's insurance policies also typically include liability coverage for dog bites, though the limits may be lower.

However, many insurance companies exclude certain breeds from coverage or charge significantly higher premiums for homes with dogs that have a history of aggression. Some policies contain specific exclusions for dog bites altogether. When a dog bite occurs on a property where the owner's insurance excludes the claim, the victim may need to pursue compensation directly from the property owner's personal assets or through other liable parties.

For apartment complexes and condominiums, the building's commercial general liability insurance may cover attacks that occur in common areas. This is a separate policy from the individual unit owner's or tenant's personal insurance, and it often carries substantially higher coverage limits. When a dog attack happens in a hallway, elevator, parking garage, pool area, or other shared space, the building's commercial policy becomes a primary source of recovery for the victim.

Common Injuries from Dog Bite Attacks

Dog bites can cause a wide spectrum of injuries, many of which require extensive medical treatment and result in permanent consequences. The most common injuries seen in Miami dog bite cases include:

  • Deep lacerations and puncture wounds — Dog teeth can tear through skin, muscle, and connective tissue, causing wounds that require surgical repair, stitches, and sometimes skin grafts
  • Serious infections — Dog bites carry a high risk of bacterial infection, including pasteurella, staphylococcus, and in rare cases, rabies. Infections can lead to hospitalization, intravenous antibiotics, and additional surgical procedures
  • Scarring and permanent disfigurement — Bite wounds frequently leave visible scars, particularly on the face, arms, and hands. Reconstructive surgery and scar revision procedures may be needed over months or years
  • Nerve damage — Bites to the hands, arms, and face can sever or damage nerves, resulting in numbness, loss of motor function, or chronic pain that may be permanent
  • Broken bones and crushed tissue — Large dogs can exert enough bite force to fracture bones in the hands, arms, and face, particularly in children and elderly victims
  • Psychological trauma — Post-traumatic stress disorder, severe anxiety around animals, nightmares, and lasting fear are common after a dog attack. Children are particularly vulnerable to long-term psychological effects that can interfere with their development and daily life

Children and Dog Bites: Heightened Duty of Care

Children are disproportionately affected by dog bite injuries. They are more likely to be bitten in the face and head due to their small stature, and their injuries tend to be more severe relative to their body size. The psychological impact on a child who has been attacked by a dog can be profound and lasting, manifesting as extreme fear of animals, difficulty sleeping, behavioral changes, and post-traumatic stress.

Under Florida premises liability law, property owners owe a heightened duty of care to children. The attractive nuisance doctrine recognizes that children may not fully appreciate dangers that adults would recognize and avoid. A dog that an adult might cautiously approach or avoid entirely may be irresistible to a young child who wants to pet it. Property owners, landlords, and HOAs who know that children frequent their property have an elevated obligation to ensure that dangerous animals are properly contained and that adequate safety measures are in place.

When a child is bitten by a dog on a property where children regularly play, visit, or live, the property owner's failure to address a known dangerous animal carries even greater legal weight. Juries in Miami-Dade County are particularly sympathetic to child victims of dog attacks, and the damages awarded in these cases reflect the severity of the harm and the vulnerability of the victim.

Evidence to Preserve After a Dog Bite on Someone's Property

Building a strong dog bite premises liability claim requires preserving critical evidence as quickly as possible. The following steps and documentation can make the difference between a successful recovery and a denied claim:

  • Animal control reports — Contact Miami-Dade Animal Services immediately to report the bite. An official animal control report creates a government record of the incident and triggers an investigation into the dog's vaccination status and history
  • Photographs of your injuries — Take detailed photos of all bite wounds, bruising, swelling, and any torn clothing from the day of the attack and throughout your recovery process as wounds heal and scars develop
  • Property pet policies — Obtain copies of the apartment lease, condo association rules, or HOA bylaws that govern pets on the property. These documents establish what rules existed and whether they were being enforced
  • Prior complaints about the dog — Request records of any previous complaints filed with the landlord, property manager, or HOA regarding the dog that attacked you. A history of complaints that went unaddressed strengthens the premises liability claim significantly
  • Witness statements — Get the names and contact information of anyone who witnessed the attack, as well as neighbors who may have knowledge of the dog's prior aggressive behavior
  • Medical records — Keep complete records of all emergency room visits, surgeries, follow-up appointments, prescriptions, physical therapy, and mental health treatment related to the attack
  • Surveillance footage — Many apartment buildings and condominiums have security cameras in common areas. Request that this footage be preserved immediately, as it is routinely overwritten within days or weeks

Steps to Take After a Dog Bite on Someone Else's Property

1. Seek Immediate Medical Attention

Your health is the first priority. Dog bites carry a serious risk of infection, and wounds that appear minor on the surface may involve deep tissue damage. Go to the emergency room or an urgent care facility. Follow all treatment recommendations and attend every follow-up appointment. Gaps in medical treatment are routinely exploited by insurance companies to argue that your injuries were not as serious as claimed.

2. Report the Attack to Animal Control

Contact Miami-Dade Animal Services to file an official report. This creates a formal government record of the incident and may result in the dog being quarantined to verify its vaccination status. The animal control report also documents the dog owner's identity and address.

3. Report the Incident to the Property Owner or Manager

Notify the landlord, property management company, or HOA in writing that a dog attack occurred on the property. Request that they create an incident report and preserve any surveillance footage from the area where the attack took place. Keep a copy of your written notice.

4. Document Everything

Photograph your injuries, the location where the attack occurred, any broken fences or gates that may have allowed the dog to reach you, and any signs or lack of warning signs about dogs on the property. Collect contact information from witnesses. Save all medical bills, receipts, and records of missed work.

5. Do Not Give Recorded Statements

The property owner's insurance company will likely contact you. They may seem sympathetic, but their objective is to minimize or deny your claim. Do not provide a recorded statement or sign any documents before speaking with a dog bite premises liability attorney.

6. Contact an Experienced Dog Bite Attorney

A dog bite lawyer in Miami who understands premises liability law can identify all responsible parties, send preservation letters to prevent the destruction of evidence, investigate the dog's history and the property's enforcement of pet policies, and pursue full compensation from every available insurance policy.

Statute of Limitations for Dog Bite Claims in Florida

Under Florida law, you generally have two years from the date of the dog bite to file a personal injury lawsuit. This deadline applies to claims against the dog owner as well as premises liability claims against landlords, property management companies, and HOAs. Missing this deadline typically results in the permanent loss of your right to pursue compensation.

However, the practical deadline is much shorter than two years. Evidence disappears, witnesses forget details, surveillance footage is overwritten, and property records can be altered or discarded. The sooner you involve an attorney, the more effectively your case can be investigated and documented while the evidence is fresh and available.

If the dog bite occurred on government-owned property, such as a public park or government housing, shorter notice requirements may apply. Consulting an attorney promptly ensures you do not miss any critical deadlines that could jeopardize your claim.
